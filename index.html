03 AI FOR DAILY LIFE & WORK

The everyday
toolkit.

The point of AI in daily life is not to marvel at it — it's to quietly give you back an hour a day. Here's how to make that happen.

Start with one habit

The biggest mistake with AI tools is trying to use them for everything at once. The move that works is to pick one recurring task in your week — writing meeting notices, summarising committee minutes, replying to student queries — and use AI for that task only, every single time, for two weeks. Once it's a habit, add a second task. The tools reward consistency, not enthusiasm.

Writing: draft, don't dictate

Think of AI as an intern who types very fast but doesn't yet know your voice. Never send what it writes verbatim. The pattern is: give it context, ask for a draft, then edit for tone and truth. A one-minute edit on a good draft beats a fifteen-minute struggle with a blank page every time.

  • Formal letters and notices — give it the recipient, the purpose, the tone (formal / warm / firm), and any facts it must include.
  • Replies to long emails — paste the email and say "draft a warm one-paragraph reply that confirms the meeting and asks two clarifying questions."
  • WhatsApp forwards to the department — "make this shorter, remove any repetition, keep the two dates and the venue."

Reading: summarise before you read

For any long document — a policy update, a research paper, a proposal — the fastest way to decide whether to read it fully is to have AI summarise it first. NotebookLM is the tool for this: it grounds every answer in the document you uploaded, and refuses to invent things that aren't there. This is a critical property for academic work.

Speaking: dictate, then tidy

Voice-to-text on the phone has quietly become excellent. The workflow: dictate rough notes while walking or between meetings, then paste into an AI tool with the instruction "clean this up, fix grammar, keep my meaning exactly." Ten minutes of dictation becomes a polished half-page in under a minute.

Teaching: from dense to simple

Turning a paper's dense paragraph into three plain sentences for undergraduates is a task AI does very well, provided you keep control of the accuracy. Always read the simplified version against the original before using it in class — the tool is fast, not infallible.

What to avoid

Never paste truly confidential material — unpublished research data, student marks, personal identifiers — into a free public AI tool. The safe rule is: if you wouldn't send it in an ordinary email to a colleague, don't send it to an AI either. For those cases, use enterprise or workspace-tier tools with data protection guarantees.

04 AI FOR RESEARCH & REPORTS

The research
pipeline.

From a blank page to a submitted paper, there are five stages. AI helps at every stage, but the shape of the help — and the risks — are different at each one.

Stage 1 — Search & discovery

The old workflow: keyword-search Google Scholar, wade through titles, download PDFs, hope for the best. The new workflow uses semantic search — you describe your research question in plain sentences and the tool finds papers whose meaning matches, not just their keywords. Start with Elicit, Consensus, or Semantic Scholar. They index tens of millions of papers and rank by relevance to your question.

Pair this with Google Scholar and PubMed, which remain the gold standard for citation completeness. AI-first search is faster; traditional search is more complete. Do both.

Stage 2 — Reading & extraction

This is where NotebookLM changes the game. You upload up to fifty PDFs into a single notebook, and it becomes a research assistant that only answers from those documents — with citations pointing to the exact paragraph. Ask "which of these papers used a randomised block design?" or "summarise the yield-improvement claims across all these studies." It's grounded, not guessing.

For deeper extraction — pulling sample sizes, effect sizes, or specific findings into a table — Elicit is purpose-built. Its data extraction accuracy is high, but always verify critical numbers against the original paper before you cite them.

Stage 3 — Drafting

The rule for scholarly writing with AI is simple and firm: AI drafts, you write. Never submit AI-generated text as your own without substantial rewriting in your voice. The workflow that works is:

  • Give the tool your outline, your key findings, and a few paragraphs of your existing writing so it learns your voice.
  • Ask it to draft one section at a time — introduction, methods, discussion — with specific guidance on length and emphasis.
  • Rewrite every sentence in your own words. The AI's first draft is your scaffold, not your voice.
  • Every claim in your final paper must trace back to a source you have personally read.

Stage 4 — Data, tables & figures

For statistical analysis, AI is best used as a translator — you know what analysis you want; AI helps you get the syntax right in R, Python, or SPSS. For charts, Google Sheets with Gemini can build good ones from a plain-English description. For anything that will appear in a peer-reviewed paper, a professional plotting library (matplotlib, ggplot2) still gives you the control you need.

For explaining what your statistics mean, ask an AI to translate your output into plain language — "explain what this p-value and this effect size actually mean, in words I'd use in a viva." Then you verify the explanation against your own understanding.

Stage 5 — Polish & submission

Grammarly for grammar. AI tools for restructuring paragraphs and tightening argument flow. Zotero for reference management and formatting citations to any journal's style. For the final read-through, print the paper. Reading on paper catches errors that never come up on screen — some habits pre-date the tools for good reason.

The integrity line

Most journals now ask you to disclose AI use. The honest rule: any tool that helped you find, understand, or polish is fine and worth disclosing briefly. Any tool that wrote sentences you didn't rewrite in your own words has crossed a line. When in doubt, disclose more, not less.

05 BUILDING YOUR APP

From prompt
to production.

AI-assisted coding lets you build a real, working, live-on-the-internet app in plain English — no computer-science degree required. Here's the shape of what you'll be doing.

The core idea

Every real app is three layers: a database that holds the information (projects, students, seeds, equipment), a backend that reads and writes to it safely, and a frontend — the screens you actually see and touch. AI-assisted coding tools like Claude Code write the backend and frontend for you from plain-English instructions. Supabase gives you a real, production-grade database without needing to run one yourself. GitHub keeps every version of your code safe. Vercel takes your code and makes it a live website. Together, that's the whole stack.

Why this stack, not no-code

No-code tools like AppSheet are a fine shortcut, but they cap out fast — you inherit their templates, their limits, their pricing. AI-assisted coding produces real code you own outright, that can do anything a professional developer's app can do, and that costs nothing beyond free tiers at your scale. The gap between "no-code" and "real software" has nearly closed — what used to take a development team now takes a clear description and a patient hour.

The build sequence

  • Model the world — decide what information you need to track. For a PIMS-style app: projects, principal investigators, budget, dates, status, deliverables, notes.
  • Set up your tools — Claude Code as your coding partner, GitHub to save every version, Supabase as your real database.
  • Build the data model — real tables in Supabase, properly related: projects, people, tasks.
  • Generate the screens — describe the add, edit, view, and dashboard screens in plain English; Claude Code builds them, connected to your real data.
  • Polish and test — styling, mobile-friendliness, and stress-testing with real data.
  • Ship it — push to GitHub, deploy on Vercel, and secure your Supabase data for the public internet.

What to build first

A UHS-PIMS-style Project Information Management System is the anchor project. Start with a scoped, personal version — the projects you personally supervise, the deliverables you personally track — and demonstrate value before proposing a department-wide roll-out. A small, working, personal prototype is worth more than a large, planned, unfinished institutional system.

The realistic scope

In six weeks of one-hour sessions, you will have a real, working, live prototype that you personally use, hosted on Vercel with a real Supabase database behind it. A full institutional deployment — multi-department, hosted on university infrastructure, security-reviewed, integrated with existing systems — is a different, larger project. It's a natural next step once your prototype has proven the concept.
